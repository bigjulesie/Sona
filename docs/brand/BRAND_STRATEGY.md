# Sona Brand Strategy

*A living document for the design team, copywriters, and anyone who shapes how Sona appears in the world. The tone of this document is the voice we are building toward — direct, unhurried, precise.*

---

## 1. Brand Essence

### Brand Truth

Sona exists because human intelligence — the full, irreducible combination of personality and knowledge — has never been truly portable until now.

A single sentence: **Sona makes a person present.**

Not their data. Not a summary. Not a transcript. The person — how they think, what they know, how they'd respond — present in the room, even when they can't be.

### Brand Promise

**To creators:** Your whole self — your thinking, your expertise, your way of seeing the world — becomes something people can carry with them. You are no longer limited by time, geography, or calendar availability.

**To subscribers:** The people who shape how you think are no longer out of reach. Whether they're a public intellectual, a mentor, a scientist in a niche you're exploring, or someone whose mind you admire from a distance — they're present when you need them.

### The Core Tensions Sona Holds

Sona is defined by three productive paradoxes. These are not problems to solve — they are the brand's substance. Every piece of communication should hold them with confidence.

**Personality and knowledge are inseparable.** A knowledge base without a person behind it is a search engine. A personality without knowledge is entertainment. Sona is neither. The fusion is the product. When someone asks a question, they don't get an answer extracted from a document — they get the answer *that person* would give, shaped by how they think, what they value, and what they know.

**Presence without being present.** A creator's Sona is available at 3am, in three conversations simultaneously, in perpetuity. This is not a diminishment of the creator — it is an extension. The brand does not hide this paradox or frame it as a limitation overcome. It frames it as the invention: the ability to be present even when you can't be.

**Intimate and infinitely scalable.** A subscriber's relationship with a Sona feels one-on-one. It is not a broadcast, a newsletter, a feed. It is a conversation. And yet one creator can have ten thousand subscribers having that conversation simultaneously. Sona holds both truths: the intimacy is real, and the scale is real.

## 2. Positioning

### Category Definition

Sona does not belong in the AI assistant category. It does not belong in the knowledge management category. It does not belong in the creator monetisation category — though it encompasses elements of all three.

Sona creates a new category: **persistent presence.**

Persistent presence is distinct from AI chat (which is transactional and general), distinct from content (which is passive and broadcast), and distinct from access products (which simulate scarcity). It is the ongoing, interactive availability of a specific person's mind — their accumulated knowledge fused with their irreplaceable personality — available not once, not on demand in a limited window, but continuously, without end.

The category Sona is building: **the AI persona economy.**

### Competitive Landscape

**Character.ai / Replika:** Entertainment-first. Characters are fictional, aspirational, or parasocial. There is no knowledge layer, no grounding in real expertise, no creator economy. The relationship is the product, not the intelligence.

**Notion AI / Guru / knowledge bases:** Knowledge-first, personality-absent. These are tools for retrieval. They do not reflect a person — they index content. The human is invisible.

**Cameo / Masterclass:** Creator economy, but passive and asynchronous. Cameo sells moments. Masterclass sells recordings. Neither creates ongoing presence or genuine interactivity. You consume the creator — you cannot converse with them.

**ChatGPT / Claude (with custom instructions or GPTs):** General-purpose, not persona-native. GPTs and custom assistants approximate a voice but are not grounded in an individual's actual knowledge, IP, and expressed personality. There is no creator economy, no tier system, no ingestion pipeline.

**Substack:** The closest structural analogue (see below). But Substack is still a publishing product. Content is produced and consumed. Sona is not a publishing product — it is a presence product.

Sona's differentiation is not a feature advantage — it is a category claim. Nobody else is doing this, which means Sona must name the category, own it, and make every competitor look like a partial answer.

### The Substack Analogy — Expanded

To understand what Sona is doing, it helps to understand what Substack did.

Before Substack, individual writers existed in one of two structures: institutional (employed by a publication, writing within its editorial frame) or informal (a personal blog, invisible and unmonetised). Substack did not invent writing. It did not invent email. It invented the infrastructure that let a single person's voice become a self-sustaining economic unit — with direct payment, direct audience ownership, no platform intermediary extracting all the value.

The result: a new class of professional emerged. Not an employee of a media company. Not an amateur blogger. An independent writer with a direct subscriber relationship, a business model, and ownership of their own intellectual output.

Sona is doing this for human intelligence itself.

Before Sona, an individual's knowledge and personality existed in one of two structures: embodied (fully present, limited by time and geography) or mediated (books, interviews, courses — static, consumed, not interactive). There was no infrastructure for a person to make their intelligence directly available, interactively, at scale, with creator ownership and monetisation.

Sona builds that infrastructure.

What Substack did for the written word, Sona does for the mind behind it. The creator does not produce content — they instantiate themselves. The subscriber does not consume content — they engage with a presence.

This is not a metaphor. It is the product.

### Positioning Statement

For intellectually curious people who want more than content — subscribers seeking genuine engagement with specific individuals, and creators whose expertise and personality deserve to travel further — Sona is the AI persona platform that makes a person's mind interactively present, because it fuses real knowledge with real personality in a way no other platform can.

## 3. Audience Personas

### The Creator

**Who they are.** They have spent years, often decades, building expertise. They are writers, scientists, engineers, coaches, academics, artists, consultants, or professionals whose thinking has genuine value. They are not necessarily famous — but they are known in their world. They have accumulated knowledge that lives in documents, conversations, papers, books, and years of practice. They have a way of thinking that is distinctly theirs.

**What they want.** To have their ideas reach further than a one-to-one conversation allows. To build a relationship with an audience that is not dependent on posting constantly, showing up live, or writing another newsletter. To be known for their thinking, not just their output. To create something that generates ongoing value from the expertise they have already built.

**What they fear.** That they will be replaced by generic AI — their specific thinking commoditised away. That platforms will extract their value and own their audience. That building an AI version of themselves requires surrendering control over how they are represented. That the technology will make them feel reduced rather than extended.

**What Sona offers them.** A platform designed from the ground up around the individual, not the content. Their Sona is theirs — grounded in their actual words, their actual knowledge, their actual IP. The tier system rewards investment: the more they contribute, the richer the Sona becomes, and the more value their subscribers receive. They own their audience relationship. They set their price. They are not a contributor to a platform — they are the product.

### The Subscriber

**Who they are.** They are learners, admirers, professionals, and fans who have a specific person they want to understand more deeply. They follow people, not topics. They have purchased books, attended talks, and listened to interviews — but those touchpoints are always one-way. They know how a person communicates publicly but not how they think privately, how they would respond to a specific situation, or how they would approach a problem the subscriber is facing right now.

**What they want.** Real engagement with the people who shape how they think. Not a curated feed, not another summary — a genuine conversation. The ability to ask the question they would ask if they had an hour with this person. To learn from someone's expertise and perspective in a form that responds to their specific context.

**What they fear.** That the Sona is just a chatbot wearing a person's name. That the intimacy is fake — a product designed to simulate a relationship for retention. That their time spent engaging with a Sona is somehow hollow compared to a real interaction with the person.

**What Sona offers them.** Genuine grounding. A Sona built on real knowledge, real IP, real personality — not a generic model with a custom persona. The Discovery tier lets them test whether a specific Sona is worth their investment before committing. As they go deeper into the tier system, the intelligence becomes richer and more specific. And the group presence feature changes the frame entirely: this is not a chatbot you consult — it is a presence you can bring into your world.

### The Marketplace Flywheel

Creator quality determines subscriber interest. Subscriber interest funds creator investment. Creator investment deepens the Sona. A deeper Sona attracts more subscribers. This is the flywheel.

The launch-phase risk is the cold-start problem: no creators, no subscribers; no subscribers, no creators. Sona solves this by making the free tier genuinely useful for creator exploration — the cost to create a basic Sona is zero, so early adopters can participate without economic commitment. Every creator who creates a free Sona is a potential advocate. Every subscriber who discovers a Sona on the free tier is a potential paying customer.

The platform's job in the first twelve months is to make the flywheel visible: showcase creators whose Sona is delivering real value to subscribers, and make it legible how subscriber growth translates to creator revenue.

## 4. Brand Architecture

### What Is a Sona — Canonical Definition

A Sona is an interactive AI persona grounded in the personality and knowledge of a specific real person.

It is not a chatbot configured to sound like someone. It is not a knowledge base with a name attached. It is the result of deliberately ingesting how a person thinks — through interviews, through their written and recorded work, through their expertise — and making that accessible as an ongoing, interactive presence.

For all brand communications, this definition is the standard. A Sona is always a person. It is always interactive. It is always a fusion of personality and knowledge. If a piece of copy does not hold all three, it is describing something else.

### Tier Narrative Arc

The tier system is not a pricing ladder. It is a depth arc. Each tier represents a different level of access to a person's mind — and should be communicated as such.

**Discovery (free).** This is the introduction. A subscriber encounters the Sona for the first time, asks initial questions, gets a sense of the person's voice and thinking. It is enough to know whether this is a mind worth going deeper with. For the creator, Discovery is the invitation — the threshold that any subscriber can cross without friction.

**Perspective (entry paid tier).** The Sona deepens. Subscribers gain access to knowledge layers built from the creator's professional body of work, their published thinking, their expressed perspectives on their domain. This is where the professional value begins — where a subscriber can ask questions and receive answers grounded in real expertise, not just public persona.

**Wisdom (mid tier).** The Sona reflects accumulated experience. Content at this tier includes more personal, more nuanced, more complex dimensions of the creator's thinking. The creator has invested significantly in the Sona — through extended interviews, deeper IP ingestion, more detailed personal and professional material. The subscriber is not just learning from someone's expertise; they are learning how that person developed it.

**Legacy (highest tier).** This is the complete mind. Reserved for creators who have contributed the fullest possible depth of their knowledge and personality — and for subscribers who want the deepest access available. At this tier, the Sona is not just a presence; it is an archive of a life's thinking, fully interactive.

The brand story that holds this arc together: a Sona grows. It is not a static product delivered once — it is a living system that becomes richer as the creator invests in it and as the subscriber goes deeper. The platform's narrative should always reflect this growth quality: a Sona is never finished.

### The Free Tier as Brand Acquisition

The free tier is not a degraded product. It is the front door.

Every Discovery-tier Sona that a subscriber engages with is a demonstration of the category itself. The free tier should deliver enough genuine value that the subscriber understands what a Sona is before being asked to pay. The upgrade moment should feel like natural depth — not a paywall in front of the real product.

For the brand, the free tier does something critical: it populates the explore page with real Sonas, real creators, real variety. The platform looks alive from day one. The risk of a paid-only model at launch is that the explore page is empty. The free tier prevents this.

Communicate the free tier generously. Never make a creator feel that creating for free is settling. The Discovery tier is the beginning of a creator's Sona journey — the foundation everything else is built on.

## 5. Voice and Tone

### Brand Personality

**Precise.** Sona does not pad. It does not hedge. It does not use five words where two will do. Precision is not coldness — it is respect. Every word assumes the reader is intelligent and attentive. In practice: active sentences, short paragraphs, no filler phrases ("We're excited to announce that..."), no throat-clearing.

**Editorial.** Sona has a point of view. It speaks with the confidence of a publication that knows what it believes. This is not arrogance — it is the natural voice of a platform built around exceptional individuals. In practice: declarative statements, not questions posed at the reader; claims made without hedging; the product positioned without apology.

**Intimate.** Sona is not a platform you use — it is a presence you access. The voice should feel close, not institutional. It addresses the reader directly and personally. In practice: second person throughout, "your circle" not "your subscriptions," names used where known, no collective nouns for users ("users," "customers," "community").

**Unhurried.** Sona does not create urgency through pressure. There are no countdown timers, no FOMO mechanics, no "limited time" warnings. The value is self-evident and permanent. In practice: no exclamation marks, no urgency language, no promotional superlatives. Let the product make the case.

**Grounded.** Sona's claims are specific, not aspirational. It does not promise to change the world — it describes something real that it does. In practice: concrete language over abstract ("ask them anything" over "unlock limitless knowledge"), product-led statements over vision-led statements in UI copy.

### Voice Principles

**Sona does not explain itself.** The product is novel. The instinct is to over-explain. Resist it. A brief, confident description is more compelling than a thorough one. If someone needs to read three paragraphs to understand what Sona is, the copy has failed.

**Sona speaks about people, not technology.** The AI is the infrastructure. The person is the product. Every sentence that foregrounds the technology ("our AI model," "advanced language processing") is a sentence that deprioritises what actually matters. Write about the person, the mind, the presence.

**Sona does not apologise.** Error states are factual. "Something went wrong — try again." Not "We're so sorry! Something unexpected happened on our end." Status messages are declarative. The product treats the user as capable of handling honest information.

**Sona does not use the language of AI products.** No: "AI-powered," "smart," "intelligent assistant," "chatbot," "digital twin," "synthetic human," "virtual." Yes: Sona, presence, mind, voice, expertise, knowledge, personality, conversation.

### Tone Variations

**Creator acquisition** (landing page, onboarding, creator-facing marketing). Confident, direct, ambitious. The tone communicates that Sona understands the creator's worth and has built something equal to it. There is no sell — just a clear account of what is possible. "Your whole self, present when it matters." Not "Reach more people with AI."

**Subscriber conversion** (explore, Sona profile, subscribe gate). Warm, specific, present-tense. Describe what the subscriber is about to access — not what it is in abstract, but what it feels like to be in that conversation. "Ask them anything. They're here." Not "Subscribe to access this Sona's knowledge base."

**Product UI** (dashboard, settings, onboarding steps). Precise, efficient, human. Labels are short and direct. Instructions do not over-explain. Status language is plain. "Schedule your interview" not "Complete step 3 of your Sona setup." "In review" not "Your submission is currently being processed."

**Error states.** Factual, calm, actionable. No apology. No alarm. "Unable to connect — try again in a moment." One sentence. One action. Done.

### Words Sona Owns

- Sona (the product, the persona, the thing)
- Presence / present
- Circle (subscriber's collection of Sonas)
- Mind / thinking
- Know / known ("Know them. Be known.")
- Wisdom (tier name and general)
- Interview (the creator ingestion process)

### Words Sona Avoids

- AI / artificial intelligence (use "Sona")
- Chatbot / bot / assistant
- Digital twin / virtual / synthetic (as standalone descriptors)
- Unlock (acceptable for tier language but use carefully — not in CTAs)
- Smart / intelligent / powered by
- Community / users / customers (use "creators," "subscribers," "people")
- Excited / thrilled / delighted / proud to announce
- Seamless / frictionless / intuitive (show it; don't claim it)

### Voice in Practice — Example Sentences

These are written in the voice. Read them and internalise the register before writing anything else.

"A Sona is not an archive. It is a presence — the way a person thinks, available when you need it."

"Your interview is where a Sona begins. The more you give it, the more fully it carries you."

"Subscribe to start a conversation. The first question is always the one you never got to ask."

"Remarkable minds. Some you know. Some you've always wanted to."

"Your Sona grows with every interview, every piece of work you bring to it. It is never finished. Neither are you."

## 6. Narrative Arc — Twelve Months

### Launch Narrative: The Concept Arrives

At launch, Sona's story is the concept itself. The category is new. The first job is not to convince people Sona is good — it is to make them understand what Sona is, because once they understand it, the value is self-evident.

The launch narrative foregrounds the fusion: personality plus knowledge, inseparably combined. The hero copy ("Know them. Be known.") captures both sides — subscriber and creator — without separating them. The concept draws both in before the platform bifurcates the journey.

At launch, Sona needs a small number of high-quality Sonas from known or credible creators. Not scale — depth. Three remarkable Sonas are worth more than three hundred empty ones. The explore page at launch should feel curated: each Sona a demonstration of what the category can hold.

The story to tell: *Something new is here. Not AI in general — something specific. A person, available.*

### Six-Month Milestone: The Marketplace Becomes Visible

At six months, the story shifts from the concept to the ecosystem. Creators are building. Subscribers are engaging. There are creators who have reached Perspective and Wisdom tier depth, and the quality difference is legible.

The brand story now has evidence. The six-month narrative introduces social proof — not testimonials in the traditional marketing sense, but visible depth: subscriber counts that reflect genuine engagement, creators who can speak to what building their Sona has meant for how they are known.

The platform also begins to earn its Substack analogy in full. Substack's cultural moment arrived not when it launched but when it had enough independent writers to look like a movement. Sona's equivalent is when the creator community is large enough and diverse enough that the pattern is visible: this is where thinkers come to extend themselves.

The story to tell: *People are here. Minds worth knowing. A category is forming.*

### Twelve-Month Milestone: Category Ownership

At twelve months, Sona owns the category it created. The brand story is no longer primarily about the concept — it is about the standard. Sona is what persistent presence means. Alternatives are measured against it.

The brand at twelve months can afford to be more selective in its language. It no longer needs to explain what a Sona is at length. A Sona is a known thing. The narrative moves to quality — the depth of the Sonas on the platform, the richness of the Legacy tier as a genuinely novel human achievement (a complete, interactive mind, preserved and available), and the creator economy story: people building sustainable income from their expertise in a form that did not exist before.

The twelve-month brand is also beginning to ask the harder question: what is the cultural significance of what we are building? When a great mind can remain present and interactive indefinitely, what does that mean for how we learn, how we remember, how we inherit intellectual traditions? Sona at twelve months is allowed to hold this question openly — not as a marketing claim, but as genuine inquiry the brand is willing to sit with.

The story to tell: *A new kind of presence. A new kind of permanence.*

## 7. Brand Do's and Don'ts

### Five Brand Do's

**Do let the person be the headline.** In every creator-facing page, the creator is the subject. Their name, their expertise, their way of thinking. Sona is the frame — never the subject. The most effective Sona copy is copy that makes you feel like you're already in a conversation with the person, not reading about a platform.

**Do trust the restraint.** The white space, the single accent colour, the quiet typography — these are not minimalism for aesthetics. They are a deliberate signal: Sona believes so completely in the value of each individual person that it has stripped away everything that might compete. Introduce colour, decoration, or visual complexity and you introduce noise where there should be signal.

**Do use the tier names as a narrative arc, not a pricing ladder.** Discovery, Perspective, Wisdom, Legacy — these names tell a story of depth. Communication about the tiers should reflect this: not "what you get at each level" but "how deep you go." The subscriber is on a journey, not selecting a package.

**Do write for the specific person, not the general audience.** Sona's value is specificity. Generic copy about "accessing knowledge" or "learning from experts" is category copy — it could describe any number of products. Every piece of Sona copy should feel like it is written for the person holding a specific creator in mind, wanting a specific kind of access.

**Do hold the paradoxes with confidence.** The brand lives in the tension between presence and absence, between intimate and scalable, between the real person and the AI. Do not resolve these tensions in copy. Do not explain them away. Name them plainly. "A Sona is available when the person can't be." This is not a limitation — it is the invention.

### Five Brand Don'ts

**Don't position Sona as an AI product.** The AI is invisible infrastructure. Every time the copy foregrounds the technology ("AI-powered," "built with advanced language models"), it misplaces the subject. The subject is always the person. Sona is a presence product. The category is persistent presence, not AI.

**Don't imply the Sona is a substitute for the real person.** A Sona is an extension — a way for a creator to be present beyond the limitations of their physical availability. The copy must never suggest that a Sona replaces the real person or is equivalent to them. "The right mind in the room" — not "just like talking to them in person." The distinction matters for trust.

**Don't use urgency mechanics.** No countdown timers, no "limited spots available," no "act now" framing. These mechanics work against the brand's signal. Sona communicates that its value is permanent and self-evident. Urgency implies scarcity that has to be manufactured — Sona's value is not manufactured.

**Don't let the explore page look like a content library.** Each Sona is a jewel. The browse experience must feel curated and respectful of each individual — not a grid of tiles optimised for quantity. As the creator base grows, curation becomes more important, not less. The brand's visual language on the explore page must always signal that each person there is worth knowing.

**Don't explain the product in the product.** Once a user is inside Sona — in the dashboard, in a conversation, in their account — the copy should not explain what Sona is. That work is done. In-product copy is operational: what to do next, what has happened, what is needed. Over-explanation in the product signals a lack of confidence. The product demonstrates what it is by being what it is.

### Common Positioning Traps to Avoid

**The AI assistant trap.** The instinct to position Sona alongside ChatGPT, Copilot, and similar tools because they share surface-level technology. Sona is not an AI assistant. It does not help you write emails or summarise documents. The moment Sona competes in the general AI assistant category, it loses. Stay in the category you created.

**The digital twin trap.** "Digital twin" is adjacent to what Sona is, but it carries clinical, uncanny connotations — simulation, replica, copy. Sona is not a copy of a person. It is a presence grounded in a person. The language of copying implies replacement. The language of presence implies extension. Never use "digital twin" in any brand communication.

**The content platform trap.** Framing Sona as "content" — even "interactive content" or "AI-powered content." Content is passive. Content is consumed. Sona is conversational, active, responsive. The moment subscribers think of a Sona as content, the product has failed to communicate its category.

**The access/exclusivity trap.** Positioning the paid tiers as exclusive access implies that the value is in scarcity — you get something others don't. This is the wrong frame. The value is in depth, not exclusivity. A subscriber at the Wisdom tier has more access to a person's thinking because that person has invested more in making it available — not because they are one of a limited number admitted. Depth, not gatekeeping.

**The fan engagement trap.** There is real overlap between Sona's subscriber base and the audience for celebrity or creator fan products (live Q&As, exclusive content, DMs). Sona is not a fan engagement product. The relationship it enables is not fan-to-creator — it is learner-to-mind, or peer-to-peer across knowledge domains. If Sona's early creator base skews toward influencers rather than experts, the brand will drift into a category it cannot own. Protect the intellectual seriousness of the platform from the start.
